What the new dancers learned last night 
The new formation was "Cross and cast", and we learned "stepping up" and "stepping down". 

Cross and Cast: This is a way to get to the next position down (or up). 
  • Cross right hand and stay facing out. Do not hold on too long when crossing, as that may then point you in the wrong direction. 
  • Cast behind the next person down (or up) and into their place - the person whose place you are taking, will have stepped up (or down)

Stepping up: https://youtu.be/axF4GUffvGs  this is done to make room for another person having to move into their spot (as above). 
  • Four elements: Step, cross, step, close;
  • Start with the foot on the side of the direction you are going in: step. Then the other foot crosses in front, then another step in the same direction, and the other foot closes up. So, if moving to the right: step to the right with the right foot; cross the left foot in front and to the other side of the R foot; another step with the R foot; and close the L foot against the other R foot. Finish in 1st position ( i.e. feet at right angles, with the heels together) 
  • It is done on the balls of the feet.
  • Depending on the dance, one can step down or up singly, or in a row (as in the video). Sometimes one has to step up (or down) twice.
  • It takes two bars to step up. We practised it by waiting for bars 1 & 2, and stepping up on bars 3 & 4. 

We then did a warm-up walking dance which included the newly learned movements (numbers indicate number of bars): 
1-4 1st couple cross Right hand, and cast down one place into second place ; 2nd couple step up to 1st place
5-8 1s cross right hand again and cast up back to 1st place ; 2s step down back to 2nd place 
9-16 the 2nd couple repeat this set of movements: (using abbreviations:) 
9-12  2s cross RH and cast up; 1s step down
13-16 2 cross RH and cast down; 1s step up.
17-24 1st couple lead down (right hand in right) for 4 bars, lead back up for 4 bars. The 2nd couple step in behind them for:
25-32 1st and 2nd couples dance an Allemande. 

After the foot and ankle exercise, in a circle, we had another practise of the Allemande, which we learned last week. https://youtu.be/2t16DXTQLSM

Videos of steps and formations
In the last few emails (and in the next ones coming up), I attached videos of the basic formations we have learnt at class. These brilliant videos were made a few years ago by the Lower Hutt Club, and are performed by dancers ( mostly SCD teachers, actually ) who have good technique. Do look at how they use their feet, arms and hands, as well as how the formation works.

A word about technique: A great many dancers are not concerned with technique - they dance for the sheer joy of dancing, the music, the movement, the company - and that is perfectly OK. But if you are going to look at videos, you may as well look at dancing that is done ‘properly’ and learn from it. On the other hand, learning good technique (i.e. paying attention to detail) is relatively easy, but unlearning bad technique is quite hard. 


Step practise
We reviewed: 
Skip change of step (also called just “skip change”) – https://youtu.be/3-5rugdB-FY The rhythm is “Hop, step, close, step"
Catherine stressed: on the balls of your feet (no heels on the floor); the the “close” part of the step, the non-beginning foot closes behind the other at right angles; the leg you step forward on should remain straight (from the hip) and the toes pointed down.  
Slip step – a sideways step, usually done in a circle. 2 steps per bar.  https://youtu.be/1RA7iJlh5ik Seven steps to the left, slow down for the 8th (4 bars), ready to change direction; seven steps to the right, slow down, close on the 8th step (4 more bars). The feet should touch between steps.
Strathspey travelling step –  the slower tempo step. This moves forward: https://youtu.be/ykCf1wmYETk  The rhythm is “Step, close step, hop”. Step forward on the right foot (knee straight, toes pointed), left foot closes behind, and right foot forward and hop on the right foot. As you hop on the right foot, the left foot slides past the right ankle to the front, almost touching the floor, ready to stretch forward for the next step. Do not jerk the knee up as you are bringing the foot past the other ankle, keep the leg just slightly bent as you slide it to the front, and keep the toes pointed down (not up!)
Strathspey setting step – This is similar to the travelling step but it moves sideways. Step to the right, close with the left foot behind the right, and step to the right again, then hop on the right foot, while bringing the left foot up behind the right ankle, toes pointing down (R, L, R, hop right). Then move to the left: step, close, step, hop (L, R, L, hop left) . A setting step is used to acknowledge the person you are “setting to”. Note that when setting in a dance, both sides move towards their right first, so the two opposing people (or rows) move in opposite directions.  

Note the difference between the steps:
For Skip change of step the hop is at the start - hop, step, close, step 
For Strathspey the hop is at the end - step, close, step, hop

Last night’s dances
The new dancers did the first three dances. Then they either went home or watched the more experienced dancers for the next three dances. 
  1. Allemande to Go (R) – https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/16844/
  2. Sandy O’er the Lea (S) – https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/5889/  Catherine modified bars 17-24 to: “2s followed by 1s (rather than the other way round), lead down the middle, and back up”. Note that doing an Allemande in strathspey time is much slower than you’d expect. Listen to the music’s rhythm and slow down.
  3. Lady Catherine Bruce’s Reel (J) – This is a 2- couple dance, which we danced in 3-couple sets. The Allemande provides the “progression”. So the “dancing couple” dances it twice. https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/3594/ The top two videos are confusing. Watch the "2014 in Lower Hutt" one, watch the set with the lady in the bright green top. 
  4. The Chequered Court (J) – This is on Waikanae’s Annual Dance programme.  https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/10787/ In the 'demonstration quality' videos, note how wide the set is, and note how the supporting dancers move forward to do the double triangles and back to the sides. 
  5. Cherrybank Gardens (S) – This is on the Ashhurst dance programme. https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/1176/   A 3C dance in a 3C set, devised by John Drewry. Lots of turning to the right - somewhat dizzying. 
  6. The Reel of the Royal Scots (R) – This is the final dance on the Waikanae programme. A great dance to finish the evening. https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/5529/ There was some discussion as to what occasion this dance was devised for: Roy Goldring was commissioned to devise this dance to celebrate the 350th anniversary in 1983 of Scotland’s oldest regiment.

NZ Branch Summer School – 28 December - 5 January
As you will know by now, this year, Summer School will be hosted by the Wellington Region. This means that as Wellingtonians, we will have the choice between attending as “residents” (i.e. including accommodation at Scots College, and all meals), or as “commuters” (which includes classes and all evening events). As a commuter, you will save $1,100 on the normal cost of attending Summer School. This is a big opportunity for people who don’t usually go to Summer School.
