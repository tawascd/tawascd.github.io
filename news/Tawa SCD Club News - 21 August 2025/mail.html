Tonight's dances
The first four dances were done with the new dancers. The last three are on the programme of the Johnsonville-Capital City Annual Dance
  1. All Right (R) - Only the R hand is used, except for the Allemande 
  2. Allemande to Go (R) - Devised by Lower Hutt teacher Gaye Collin. We did this dance last week. https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/16844/
  3. Sandy O'er the Lea (S) - We also did this last week, slightly modified: in bars 17-24, 2s followed by 1s lead down, 1s follow 2s back up, to make it easier for the new dancers. https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/5889/
  4. Lady Catherine Bruce's Reel (J) - https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/3594/
  5. The Abbot of Unreason (J ) - This dance finishes with a circle with a difference: See on the R foot, slip step to the left for 7 bars; set on the L foot, slip step to the right for 7 bars. For the transition, make sure you finish the last slip step, and sift your weight on to the R foot, ready to hop for the Pas de Basque. https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/9286/ . See below for some interesting info about the title.
  6. The Bon Viveur (M) - This is a medley, meaning the dance goes through twice as a strathspey, then twice as a reel. It is danced in a square set, in which couples move along one position each time through. https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/16838/  It includes a men's chain, and 12 bars of a Schiehallion Reel.  https://youtu.be/hVeNDKJJwOk The title of the dance is "pseudo-French" for someone who enjoys the good things in life (correct French would be "bon vivant"). 
  7. Roaring Jelly (J) - By this time of the evening we only had seven couples left, so we danced it as a "beg and borrow" set. https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/5621/


The Abbot of Unreason
This dance is on the Johnsonville Annual Dance programme (it is one of Rod Downey's favourite dances). Robert Vale, who is the Johnsonville secretary, usually writes a witty or interesting item about a dance done at a club night, and this week he wrote about The Abbot of Unreason. I hope Robert won't mind my copying it here (I haven't had a chance to ask him, but he usually doesn't mind). Thank you Robert! 

One of Monday night's jigs was The Abbot of Unreason. The short explanation is an abbot is in charge of a monastery, the buildings of a religious community. 
It gets more complicated. A Christian monastery may be an abbey (i.e., under the rule of an abbot), or a priory (under the rule of a prior), or conceivably a hermitage (the dwelling of a hermit). It may be a community of men (monks) or of women (nuns). 
The Abbot of Unreason has little to do with these religious organisations. In the Middle Ages and into the Elizabethan period, the celebrations of Easter, May Day, Whitsuntide, Midsummer Eve and Hallowe'en were regarded as times of Misrule, with the Twelfth Night after Christmas the biggest and most unruly celebration of the lot.
Misrule meant a period of anarchy when the normal rules didn't apply. It was a chance to let off steam, challenge convention and be rude. The hierarchy was tipped on its head - jesters became kings, men dressed as women, masters served their servants.
Because Christmas was the peak period of Misrule, a Lord of Misrule was appointed to manage the Christmas festivities held at Court and in the houses of great noblemen, the law schools of the Inns of Court, and even in many of the colleges at the universities of Cambridge and Oxford.
During his reign, which lasted anywhere from 12 days to 3 months, the Lord of Misrule was responsible for arranging and directing all Christmas entertainment, including elaborate masques and processions, plays, and feasts. The Lord himself usually presided over these affairs with a mock court and received comic homage from the revellers.
And what does any of this have to do with our dance? Well, Scotland had a similar character, known as the Abbot of Unreason. The role was suppressed at the Reformation in 1560, the official end of fun in Scotland.
