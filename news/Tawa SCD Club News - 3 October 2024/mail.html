Last night’s dances
Since I wasn’t there last night, I’m assuming the new dancers did numbers 1 to 5, and the last three will have been for the more seasoned members. 

  1. Short and Very Sweet (J) –This will have been the walking warm-up dance. Devised in 2004 by Rod Downey for Isla Norris, a very longstanding member of the Johnsonville  Club, for her 90th birthday. Isla was very tiny, and very sweet. https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/12568/
  2. The March Hare (R) – There are two dances with this name. I suspect that it was Iain Boyd’s dance that you did. https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/4142/
  3. Everything Stops for Tea (S) – Would this be one of next week’s dances? With a “dessert” theme? https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/8855/
  4. The Kissing Bridge (R) – Devised by Wellington teacher and deviser Romaine Butterfield. A kissing bridge is a North American covered bridge - good, perhaps, for a ’stolen kiss’, away from prying eyes? I imagine that Catherine will have taught the “poussette” and the “under the arm” turn for the new dancers, before teaching this dance. This dance is very popular in the Wellington region. It is on the programme for the New Dancers’ Celebration (NDC).  https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/14977/
  5. Anderson’s Rant (R) – Another chance to practise the “cross-over reels”. This dance is also on the NDC programme. https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/140/ My two pennies’ worth: Be sure to get neatly back to the sides after the “down the middle and up”, or else you’ll have a spare bar or two, and will launch into the Half Fig 8 up around the 2s too soon.  
  6. Balmoral Strathspey (S) – This dance includes reels of four on the sides. It is on the NDC programme. https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/373/  Balmoral is the summer residence of the Royal family. In the extra info on the link, there is mention of the annual "Ghillies Ball”, held at Balmoral. Here is a (quite old) clip of the royal family dancing the Eightsome Reel at Balmoral: https://youtu.be/byWrelwQK8o (The Eightsome Reel is one of the highlights of Hogmanay at Summer School). 
  7. The Cranberry Tart (J) – For the first 16 bars in this dance, the lady does the same as the man, but two bars later. It’s a very popular dance, as you can tell from the number of videos available on the link. https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/9157/
  8. City of Stirling (R) – I don’t think I have ever done this dance before. It looks like fun. Though devised in 2006 by Roy Goldring, it was only published in RSCDS Book 53 last year. https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/13270/


What was new for the new dancers?

Poussette
The name comes from the French verb “pousser”, meaning “to push”. (“poussette” is French for a child’s pushchair). https://youtu.be/TEXJh0Sj-K0 
Salient points:
  • Done with two couples. 
  • Couples join both hands, held slightly wider than shoulder width. Firm arms - no “spaghetti arms”. 
  • It is a progression, i.e. when done, the two couples are in reversed places
  • It is danced in a square. Each couple dances half of the square: the 1s dance towards the men’s side to begin, the 2s dance towards the ladies’ side.
  • The “jingle” that goes with this formation is: "Away from the centre, quarter turn; up or down, quarter turn; into the centre, half turn round; dance back, dance back". Each of those elements takes one bar, i.e. one PdB step.  
  • The person who finds themselves in the corner just pivots on the spot, while their partner dances the quarter turn. 
  • You are either moving or turning, but never both at the same time. 

I don’t know if Catherine went into this, but from what I know of The Kissing Bridge, she most probably will have mentioned these points:

Lead down vs dance down
In The Kissing Bridge, the instructions say "1s lead down middle & cross to own sides (Lady under Mans arm), 1s lead up to top”
Lead down - means right hand in right. This is usually when the couple have to turn around and go back up.
Dance down - means nearer hands joined. This is usually when the couple have to cast to their own sides

Cross to own sides (Lady under Man’s arm)
This move often causes funny wriggles, and or twisted arms. There is usually some fumbling of hands ...The things to remember are:
  • Couple both face in, towards each other, for the turn. 
  • The man dances below the lady, towards the opposite side
  • The lady moves forward, towards the man and under his arm. She ends up on the opposite side.
The move is a little more awkward when they are holding right hands (as in “lead down” in The Kissing Bridge). It is easier if they hold nearer hands, as in “dance down”.

Last Saturday’s Glendarroch Dance
Robert kindly sent me this report of Glendarroch’s Annual Dance in Whanganui, on 28 September. Thank you Robert.  

In the past the Glendarroch Annual Dance has been held in the Carlton School Hall, but this year it moved to the more spacious hall of the Whanganui Girls’ College. 

The hall was spacious and light, with a large stage for the Scottish Saltire Band. There were ten Tawa dancers on the floor, Catherine, Fiona, Diane, Debbie, Joy, Elaine, Jo, Duncan, Brenda and Robert, plus Mary on stage playing the fiddle. There were up to five sets dancing.

We started with New Year Jig, devised by Tawa’s former tutor Maureen Robson. Fortunately, Catherine had taught us several of the dances on the programme. After ten dances there was a very welcome break for an excellent afternoon tea. Refreshed, we went on to the second part of the programme, finishing with The Aviator, followed by The De’il Amang the Tailors.

It was a great dance, and there was the added bonus that Glendarroch had cleverly arranged the date to coincide with the Whanganui model railway show.

Note: Model railways are amongst Robert’s passions, along with driving a vintage tram QE II Park in Paekakariki.
