What we need to know for Saturday's Annual Dance
Please: 
  1. Arrive a little before starting time, i.e.  7:15 pm, to be there to welcome our guests from other clubs.
  2. Bring two plates of finger food for supper. Ideally one sweet  and one savoury. Or else, two savoury.
  3. Paid-up Tawa members will get in for free
  4. Wear your name tag. I will bring the name tag hanger.
  5. Covid is still around, so do not attend if you are harbouring a cold or other lurgy. 
  6. Don't attempt, or get talked into, dancing a 'two-star' dance if you don't know it well. If you 'get lost', it spoils the dance for the whole set. It is OK to decline an invitation if you are not feeling confident about a dance. 
  7. Have fun and enjoy dancing with a range of partners, especially from other clubs. 
  8. Give a hand with the pack-up and tidy-up at the end of the night.
  9. Note: There is parking outside the hall, and across the road, but also up the road at the railway station (through the tunnel). We suggest club members park at the station, so as to leave the nearer parks for our guests.

Last Saturday's McDonalds' Family Celtic Dance
Denis and Alison, attended this dance last Saturday, as did several other members, I believe. Denis wrote the following item about the event. Thank you Denis.

Last Saturday, Alison and I, along with our daughter and her partner, attended the McDonald Family Ceilidh, and what a night of entertainment and fun it was! This was the 25th Ceilidh that Mary and Duncan had organised, and with around 80 dancers, it was a testament to their ability to run a successful Ceilidh.

Held at NgƒÅti Toa Domain Hall in Mana, with a six-piece band featuring Mary on fiddle and Duncan on drums, the dance floor was soon full. I had heard Ceilidhs could be rough, but this was not the case, as the dances were well explained and easy to pick up. With an age range of 4 to 80+, there was a dance to cater to all, with a cute Squirrels dance for the younger and Paremata Roundabout for the more adventurous.

There was even an SCD dance, the New Year Jig, first demonstrated by the many SCD dancers present and then danced by everybody.

The highlight for me was seeing the smiles on both old and young faces as they danced. I will be back next year.

Denis Spurdle
