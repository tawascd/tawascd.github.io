Last night's dances 
Numbers 1 & 2 are on Linden's Annual Dance programme, and Number 5 is on Ngaio's programme. 
  1. Kendall's Hornpipe (J) - Linden - A 2C dance. We danced it in 3C sets. Includes a Ladies' Chain. https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/3435/
  2. Inchmickery (J) - Linden - This is a 5C dance, devised by Roy Goldring. We started by learning the middle 16 bars when the 1s have got into 3rd position (2x Teapots; then 1s turn RH with the person diagonally right from them at the end of the set, then turn LH with the person diagonally left from them at the other end of the set). The whole dance is quite symmetrical, as the first 8 bars are a circle to the left, then a long cast. And the last 8 bars are a long cast  and a circle to the right. https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/9636/.  The name Inchmickery refers to a small island in the Firth of Forth, about 3km north of Edinburgh. 
  3. Indian River Strathspey (S) - This is a 3C dance, where couples are in a triangle. https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/7560/  The Indian River is in Florida, USA (check out the Extra Info). Catherine chose this dance as it is on a programme of a dance she will be attending while in Scotland. 
  4. Merlin's Thorn (J) -  Catherine told us that the deviser, Alex Gray, made an impromptu visit to the Lower Hutt Club, and taught this dance to the club. He also devised the formation with which the dances finishes - The Helix, which is a progression. Catherine taught this formation first. https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/10593/ 
  5. Shiftin' Bobbins (R) - Ngaio - A great favourite. https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/6048/ 
  6. Broadway (S) - A lovely strathspey. Bars 9-17, and bars 18-24 have Set & Link for 3C, followed by Half Diag R&L to the right. Hint: the person who is on the left of the Set & Link (who links through the middle), is not involved in the Half Diag R&L. https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/13771/ 
  7. The Hazel Tree (J) - This dance by John Drewry starts with an Espagnole, which we practised first, as it is more often danced in Strathspey time, but this was in Jig time. Another dance that Catherine will encounter when dancing in Scotland. https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/2822/

This is how Rod defined "Fugues" in the last issue of Harbour City Happenings:

"Dancing fugues are analogous to musical fugues, where some figure is repeated as a theme, perhaps with variations. Examples you might know include The Cranberry Tart, or perhaps The One O'Clock Canon, the latter being a special kind of fugue called a canon. Fugues tend to be relatively complicated, and are both a challenge to dance and a great reward when achieved. They are especially good for working on teamwork and phrasing."

Last Saturday's Annual Dance
Robert, as secretary of the Johnsonville Club, has written an account of Tawa's Annual Dance for the Johnsonville newsletter. He has agreed to let me use it here too. Thank you Robert. 

Out and About - Tawa's Annual Dance 24 May, 2025

The first club in the Wellington Region to hold its annual dance this year was Tawa. They welcomed a large group to the Ngaio Town Hall on a fine Saturday night. The chairs along the whole length of both sides of the hall were soon filled with dancers putting on their shoes to dance the night away.

On the stage to provide the music was a three-piece band of violin, accordion and double bass, played respectively by Hilary Ferral, Jason Morris and Jonny Warnes. The stage was decorated with greenery and fairy lights and draped in tartan.

After warming up our feet parading round the hall, Catherine McCutcheon, the Tawa tutor and MC for the night, soon had seven sets organised for the first dance of the evening, The Old Man of Storr, named after a pinnacle of rock on the Isle of Skye. 

People were still arriving even after the dancing had started, so by the time we got to the next dance on the programme, Joie de Vivre, there were eight sets filling the hall and eager to dance. I spotted at least eleven Johnsonville dancers.

Part-way through the first half, Catherine unexpectedly asked dancers to sit down. Johnsonville's tutor Rod Downey then took to the microphone, in his capacity as President of the RSCDS Wellington Region. He presented a Region Award to Desiree Patterson. 

Desiree is a member of both Tawa and Johnsonville and received the award for her services to Scottish country dancing over many years, including as editor of the Wellington Region's magazine, Harbour City Happenings. 

Desiree was my partner for the next dance, she said she had been totally surprised by the unexpected award, which the Region had managed to keep under a strict embargo to avoid any leaks before the night.

The first half of the programme, ten dances in all, finished with The Laird of Milton's Daughter. At this point, four tables of supper were carried out from the Town Hall's adjoining kitchen. Tawa members had been busy baking and preparing food and there was an abundance of food to sustain the dancers, who had worked up an appetite with all that exertion.

The second half of the programme was slightly shorter. It started with a reminder of times past. Last year's Tawa dance, in the same hall, had been interrupted by a fire alarm, followed by  the arrival of two fire engines and lots of firefighters. As they went about their work, dancers and musicians were evacuated to stand outside in the gentle drizzle. Fortunately, it turned out to be a false alarm. What could be more appropriate to start the second part of Tawa's 2025 Annual Dance than Fire Hose Reel? The second part of the programme included another favourite of the Tawa club, Links with St Petersburg, and the night concluded with the lively reel Trip to Timber Ridge.

Many thanks to the Tawa club for starting off the Region's dancing year of 2025 in such style.
