Tonight's dances 
A couple of tonight's dances (No. 2 & 4) are on the New Dancers' (NDC) programme. Before dancing No. 3, we practised Pas de Basque. For our newer dancers, see the description below. We had supper after five dances, then No. 6 and 8 were for the more experienced dancers,  and No. 7 was suitable for the newer dancers. 

  1. Come What May (J) - This was our walking warm-up dance. We did it last week too. https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/17900/
  2. The Barmkin (R) - This is a square set dance. It is on the NDC programme.  https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/413/ 
  3. Mrs Stewart Sinton's Reel (J) - Before we danced this, we practised Pas de Basque. The dance has two lots of "set twice" (i.e. 4 PdB). We did this dance last week as well. It is called a reel, but it is actually a jig. https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/4804/
  4. Monymusk (S) - This is on the NDC programme. https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/4646/ It's an old dance, dating back to 1786. Monymusk is a village in Aberdeenshire, Scotland. The name is Gaelic, and means "nasty, filthy bog". Not very appealing, but it is a nice dance. The "extra info" in the link provides a link to an article in "The Reel", 1954, which is quite an interesting read. 
  5. Corner Meeting (J) - Devised by Johnsonville's tutor Rod Downey, as a teaching dance to teach "corners". https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/13790/ 
  6. The Garden City Jig (J) - Devised by Carol Smith, a NZ dancer; the name refers to Christchurch, the Garden City. https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/2398/ 
  7. The Silver Grey (S) - a 3C strathspey in a 3C set. https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/7517/ 
  8. The Morrison Measure (R) - a 5C reel in a 5C set.  https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/12690/ 


Pas de Basque 
This is the fifth and last step in Scottish country dancing. The others are: Slip step, skip change of step, strathspey travelling step, and strathspey setting step. 
We practised Pas de Basque in Reel tempo, 4/4, i.e. four even beats.  
  • Catherine demonstrated the step, which has three beats and a pause on the fourth beat. 
  • Then we tried the step, starting on the right foot:  R, L, R, pause; L, R, L, pause. 
  • Place R foot down; place L foot in front of the R foot (in 3rd position, T shape) and raise the R foot off the floor; shift your weight back onto the R foot again, and raise the L foot (pause); repeat, starting with the L foot. 
  • We started stepping it out slowly, and increasing in tempo, and finally doing it to the music.  
  • I think it helps if you don't put your full weight on the foot that goes in front. Just try it as if you had a stone in your shoe, and you want to lift your foot off quickly. 
  • In the video you'll see that the dancers kick the foot forward on the last beat (this is called the "jete"), but for now it is enough to just pause on that beat, until you've got the hang of the rhythm. https://youtu.be/H0_lkdhMikI 
Pas de Basque, also called 'setting', is used as a 'greeting', so usually when you set to someone, the other person will set back to you. "Setting" once, consists of two bars - once starting on the R foot and once on the L foot.


New Dancers' Celebration
