Tonight's dances
Before dancing Corn Rigs, we practiced Pas de Basque, and the new dancers learned, and the others practiced, the poussette. See details below. 
And because tomorrow is Halloween, we did a couple of Halloween-inspired dances, No 6 and 7. 

  1. Virginia Reel (R) - This was our walking warm-up dance. https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/8162/ 
  2. The Highland Fair (J) - https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/2910/ 
  3. Corn Rigs (R) - This dance finishes with a poussette. https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/1348/ 
  4. John McAlpin (S) - We did this dance at our last club night. It includes a "meanwhile",  i.e. the women dance a slightly different sequence from the men: women set, while men stand still; women advance, while men set; women retire, while men advance; women set, while men retire. Two bars for each 'bit'.   https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/3323/ 
  5. The Merry Oddfellows (J) - This also finishes with a poussette. https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/4334/  Obviously a Johnsonville favourite, as both the videos in this link were filmed in the Johnsonville School Hall, when the club used to meet. You will see some familiar faces in both videos.
  6. Hallo Halloween (J) - This was a round-in-a-circle dance. The "Halloween trick" is that the circle round and back starts by going to the right first (usually circles go to the left first) . 
  7. Slytherin House (S) - Gorgeously spooky music, and a snake pass, for Halloween.  https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/17460/  Note that in the snake pass the "teapots" are danced on opposite ends, but with the same people. 
  8. Lady Catherine Bruce's Reel (J) - Just a quick, easy dance to finish. https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/3594/


A reminder of how Pas de Basque is danced
We practised Pas de Basque in Reel tempo, 4/4, i.e. four even beats.  
  • The step has three beats and a pause on the fourth beat. 
  • Starting on the right foot:  R, L, R, pause; L, R, L, pause. 
  • Place R foot down; place L foot in front of the R foot (in 3rd position, T shape) and raise the R foot off the floor; shift your weight back onto the R foot again, and raise the L foot (pause); repeat, starting with the L foot. 
  • I think it helps if you don't put your full weight on the foot that goes in front. Just try it as if you had a stone in your shoe, and you want to lift your foot off quickly. 
  • In the video you'll see that the dancers kick the foot forward on the last beat (this is called the "jete"), but for now it is enough to just pause on that beat, until you've got the hang of the rhythm. https://youtu.be/H0_lkdhMikI 
Pas de Basque, also called 'setting', is used as a 'greeting', so usually when you set to someone, the other person will set back to you. "Setting" once, consists of two bars - once starting on the R foot and once on the L foot.

Poussette
The name comes from the French verb "pousser", meaning "to push". ("poussette" is French for a child's pushchair). https://youtu.be/TEXJh0Sj-K0 
Salient points:
  • Done with two couples. 
  • Couples join both hands, held slightly wider than shoulder width. Firm arms - no "spaghetti arms". 
  • It is a progression, i.e. when done, the two couples are in reversed places
  • It is danced in a square. Each couple dances half of the square: the 1s dance towards the men's side to begin, the 2s dance towards the ladies' side.
  • The "jingle" that goes with this formation is: "Away from the centre, quarter turn; up or down, quarter turn; into the centre, half turn round; dance back, dance back". Each of those elements takes one bar, i.e. one PdB step.  
  • The person who finds themselves in the corner just pivots on the spot, while their partner dances the quarter turn. 
  • You are either moving or turning, but never both at the same time.
