Tonight's dances
  1. Glen Etive (R) - This was our walking warm-up dance, which Catherine had written it on the board. https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/8051/ Glen Etive is a glen (valley) in the Scottish Highlands, near Glencoe. The area is famous as the film location of James Bond 'Skyfall'  and 'Braveheart'. 
  2. Milltimber Ridge (J) - This dance was quite new to all of us. It has some tricky reels across, danced by the 1C, and first the 2M entering the reel in the middle for a Rsh reel to 1L, then the 2L entering the reel to dance LSh to 1M. It finishes with an unusual 'triangular' progression. https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/7543/ 
  3. Balmoral Strathspey (S) - We've done this a few times already, it's on our Dance programme. https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/373/ 
  4. The Montgomeries' Rant (R) - On our Dance programme. To start with, we practised the second 16 bars - the1C setting to all the corners in turn, then finishing with a 6-bar reel on the sides (on their opposite sides), and cross to own side on final two bars. https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/4641/ 
  5. May the 4th be with you (R) - This was supposed to have been on the programme for last week's club night (1 May) as it was coming up to May 4th. But as last week was cancelled due to the weather, we had it tonight. It's a dance that Catherine will do just once a year, at around this date. Devised by French teacher Antoine Rousseau, it's a clever, fun dance, though a bit dizzying ... Unfortunately there is no video. But the description is here  https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/17306/ 
  6. Neidpath Castle (S) - Our "meatball sandwich" dance, on our Dance programme. We have done this lots of times. https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/4890/ 
  7. The Reverend John MacFarlane (R) - Devised by Gary Morris, a NZ dancer, teacher and examiner, who died recently. The Rev MacFarlane conducted the first church service on the beach in Petone in 1840. https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/5555/ 
  8. The Shortfield Jig (J) - Catherine wanted to teach another dance with "Hello-Goodbye" setting in it. This dance also had some tricky half reels across, in which the dancing couple changed ends for the half reels. And it had set and link twice. We practised the HG several times first. The whole 40-bar dance was quite a brain gym. Sadly, there is no video https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/13690/ 

Tonight's dances had lots of setting in them: Mill timber Jig, had set & cross, set & cross; Montgomeries' Rant had setting to corners; May the 4th be with you had double triangles; Rev John MacFarlane had a poussette; and Shortfield Jig had Hello-Goodbye setting, and set and link. Phew! Exhausting!

Manawatu SCD Club 70th Anniversary Dance
Several of our members - Joy, Robert, Brenda, Fiona McC, Debbie S, Anne and Diane - attended this dance in Palmerston North last Saturday. I discovered Joy's photos and videos on Facebook, and I spotted Robert, so I asked him if he would share the item that I was sure he would be writing for the Johnsonville Club (in his capacity as the Johnsonville Secretary). He willingly agreed. Thank you Robert. Here is the somewhat modified (for Tawa) version, and the photo he took while he was having a 32-bar break at the bottom of the set (sporrans are very useful for keeping one's phone in!). 

On Saturday 3 May the grandly titled Manawatu Scottish Society Scottish Country Dance Club of Palmerston North held a dance to celebrate their 70 years of dancing.  

The dance began at 7:00pm at the Whakarongo Community Hall, a little way to the east of Palmerston North. After the previous two days of heavy rain and extreme wind, the dance organisers must have had an anxious few days wondering if they would have to cancel the event after so much planning. Fortunately, the day of the dance turned out fine and even sunny.

By my rough count, at least 70 people were in attendance, quite a few spectators as well as dancers. We filled the large hall, with its warm varnished particleboard walls and floor and cream painted ceiling. Round the walls were the heraldic symbols of many Scottish clans. There were lots of local dancers and quite a few from further away, including Wellington. Whanganui, Levin and Napier. Seven dancers from Tawa were there to celebrate the occasion.

On the large stage were the four-piece Saltire Scottish Dance Band,  ready to provide the music for our night of dancing. The clock moved round to 7 o'clock and the dancers were sent outside under the verandah to get organised ready for the Grand March. We were led in by piper Nicole Trewavas, a fine spectacle to start the night.

The Grand March ended and the MC for the night, Debbie Roxburgh, the President of the NZ Branch of the RSCDS, took to the stage.  We formed into sets with our partners from the march for the first dance on the programme, Maureen Robson's New Year Jig. There were seven sets on the floor and the hall felt full, with people sitting round the sides to enjoy the music, watch the dancing and catch up with old friends.

The dance programme was full of Manawatu favourites, chosen because of their connections with the club or their popularity at Manawatu dances over the past 70 years. Some like Montgomeries' Rant were from the earlier books of RSCDS dances and some from much later books, including Seton's Ceilidh Band and The Luckenbooth Brooch. 

The first half of the programme ended with The Reel of the 51st Division. We stopped for the cutting of the magnificently iced (and very tasty) Anniversary cake and then the tables were brought out for tea and a lot of conversations. There was a whole lot of good food to reward our efforts on the floor.

After the dancers had been revived with food and drink and the raffles had been drawn, the night's programme resumed with the old dance Hooper's Jig, followed by the much more recently devised Orpington Caledonians (from England) and Triple Happiness (from Japan), giving a reminder of the worldwide community of dancers to which we belong.

All too soon it was the last dance, that old favourite that has ended many a programme, The De'il Amang the Tailors, which was danced with great enthusiasm. The assembled company then formed a circle to sing Auld Lang Syne. 

We had all enjoyed a wonderful night's dancing to celebrate Manawatu's 70th anniversary in style.
