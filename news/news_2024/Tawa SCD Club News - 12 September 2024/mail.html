Tonight’s Dances
The new dancers danced numbers 1 to 5, before going home. Then the rest of us did the last three.
  1. The Auld Grey Cat (R) – This was our walking warm-up dance. We danced it last week too, so it was an easy start. It includes  ‘mirror’ reels on the sides, and an Allemande.  https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/295/
  2. Crom Allt (R) – We did this last week too. Mirror reels to start. This dance introduced the concept of “corners” for the new dancers. https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/1426/ 
  3. Cabbages and Kings (J) – Devised by Romaine Butterfield. This dance incudes turning corners, and three hands across (“Teapots”). https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/900/
  4. First Love (S) – Before dancing this, we practised dancing a circle in strathspey time, and dancing a grand chain. https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/8047/
  5. New Year Jig (J) – Devised by our former teacher Maureen Robson, as a teaching dance to teach reels of three to a children’s class. It incudes R Sh figs of 8 , and R Sh reels of 3. https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/17898/
  6. The Summer Assembly (Sq set R) – This dance will be on the programme of the President’s Ball, at Summer School.  https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/6380/
  7. Triple Happiness (S) – Devised by Japanese dancer Yoshiki Oyama. https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/17122/
  8. Rodney’s Rant (J) – Includes diagonal half Rights and Lefts, and Set and Link for three. The dance was devised by Ann Dix, who also devised Culla Bay. 


What was new for our new dancers?
Corners
Catherine explained the concept of “corners” to two of the new dancers who were not here last week, when it was first introduced with the dance “Crom Allt”.  When the dancing couple stands in 2nd  place, the person diagonally across from them on their right is their 1st corner. The person diagonally across from them on their left is their 2nd corner. In this dance, the 1s Adv & Ret (advance and retire) with their 1st corner, then turn them RH (right hand); then they Adv & Ret with their 2nd corner, and turn them LH (left hand). 

“Teapots” 
In the next dance, “Cabbages and Kings”, the dancing couple turn their 1st corner RH (right hand), then turn their corner LH (left hand”. Then comes a formation which is officially called “three hands across”, but is informally known as “teapots”. From their position in 2nd place, the dancing couple dance RH across with the end couple on their right (Lady with the 2s and Man with the 3s), and return to their place in 2nd position on the sides; then they dance LH across with the other end couple on their left (Lady with the 3s, Man with the 2s) and return to 2nd place on the sides. https://youtu.be/DDMc8B1yJ6k

Dancing a circle in Strathspey 
In contrast with fast time, which has a special step (“slip step”) for dancing a circle, strathspey uses the “strathspey travelling step", but with a ”twist”, literally. When dancing in a circle (e.g. 6 hands around, or 8 hands around), the dancer is facing into the circle (looking at the person opposite). But when dancing a circle, we go to the left first: so the legs go to the left first, and because we always start with the right foot first, this means that the R foot has to cross to the left to be able to start. This means that you have to twist from the waist, body facing forward, but lower half going to the left. Going back, i.e. dancing to the right, is no problem, as the right leg goes to the right. https://youtu.be/L_3BgigdQO4 

Grand Chain
To introduce the concept, Catherine got us to start in a large circle, each facing our partner (ladies going clockwise, men going anti-clockwise). All couples change places with their partner RH (right hand), and continuing in the same direction, they change places with the next person LH (left hand). Continue for two more changes, RH, then LH. Each hand change took two steps/bars. It works as an expanded “Rights and lefts”.

From here we got into 3-couple sets. To do a grand chain with 3 couples, we have only six changes of hands. But the formation takes eight bars! So the solution is that the 1st and 2nd hand take one bar each, while the 3rd hand takes two bars. Similarly, the 4th and 5th hand take one bar each, and the 6th hand takes 2 bars: 1+1+2+1+1+2= 8, Yay! So when counting for a grand chain, we count: "Right, left, ri-i-ight, left, right, le-e-eft". To start a grand chain in a three-couple set, the 1s face each other, the 2s face down, and the 3s face up (i.e. 2s and 3s face each other on the sides). https://youtu.be/XhkqlBZ_j7w 

We did the dance “First Love”, which included a grand chain.  

Right shoulder/Left shoulder reels
So far, when we have done reels of three, they have been “mirror reels” (also called “reflection reels”). For this, the reels of the men and of the women are mirror image of each other, the 1s going in and down, the 2s going out and up, the 3s going in and up.
  • On the men’s side, the 1M is angling his body to the left to start (in and down), 2M is angling also to the left to start (out and up): they are passing each other by the Right shoulder. The 3rd man, looking at the back of 2M and going in and up, is moving to his right, to start the reel. This is called a Right shoulder reel (R Sh). 
  • On the ladies side, the 1L is angling her body to the right (going in and down), the 2L is also angling to the right (out and up): they are passing each other by the Left shoulder. The 3rd lady, looking at the back of 2L and going in and up, is moving to her left to start the reel. This is called a Left Shoulder reel (L Sh). 
  • To sum up: in a R SH reel, 1s and 2s pass R Sh, while the 3s move to their right to start.  In a L Sh reel, 1s and 2s pass L Sh, while 3s move to their left to start. 

For the next dance, “New Year Jig”, both men and women dance Right shoulder reels, which means they are dancing in parallel, instead of mirror image. The dance was devised as a means of teaching reels, so it starts with the 1s dancing Right shoulder Figs of 8 (2s and 3s standing still), both passing the 2s by their right shoulder (i.e. moving to the left - 1st Lady goes behind 2L, 1st man goes in front of 2M). Then the Figs of 8 easily flow into Right shoulder reels. 

Phew! That was quite a lot of new stuff to absorb! Lots of new jargon to learn too. Tanya and Hatsuhi did brilliantly!  And I hope that the above is useful to longer-term dancers too. It probably won’t hurt to have details reinforced every so often.

PS – for the language nerds amongst us (I know there are a few …)
You may have noticed that I refer to Capital City’s “Deviser’s Day” (with an “e”), while their poster calls it a “Devisor’s Day”(with an “o”). Sorry to nitpick, but this is one of my big bugbears in spelling. The Oxford English Dictionary definitions are: 
Deviser (with an “e”) – a person who “devises”, devise = to plan or invent (a complex procedure, system to mechanism) by careful thought. I think devising a dance certainly involves careful thought! 
Devisor (with an “o”) – a person who leaves something, especially real estate, to someone by the terms of a will. A legal term.

Last Saturday’s Lower Hutt 70th Anniversary Dance
This was a wonderful event, very well attended, with 12 sets on the floor. Tawa contributed nearly two sets (17 dancers), plus Mary in the band, and John in attendance to take photos. We were welcomed with little dance cards, which had the programme plus some extra space to write in the name of one’s partner for each dance - a nice touch. Briefing of the dances was shared between this year’s Lower Hutt teachers, Andrea Wells and Chris Totton.
