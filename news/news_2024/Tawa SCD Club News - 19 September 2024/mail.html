Last night’s dances
The new dancers did numbers 1-4, the rest of us did the remaining three dances.

  1. New Year Jig (J) – This was our walking warm-up dance. We danced it last week. https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/17898/ 
  2. Try as we Might (R) – This dance was devised by Lower Hutt teacher Gaye Collin, as a teaching dance to introduce reels across the set. As “try as she might” she could not find an easy dance that included reels across, so she devised one. 
  3. Reel Easy (R) – This dance included lots of setting, as Pas de Basque was a new step for the new dancers tonight. https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/13827/
  4. First Love (S) – We did this dance last week. It includes a grand chain. https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/8047/
  5. The Aviator (J) –  This dance was published in Book 52. It depicts a glider being accompanied by a buzzard. Do read the ‘Extra info’ on the link.  https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/14401/   Catherine called this “Desiree’s favourite dance”, because I was inclined to avoid it, as I managed to muck it up a few times. But I think I've got it sussed now!
  6. Miss Eleanor (S) – A lovely strathspey that includes a 3C bourrel. Devised by Ann Dix who also devised Culla Bay and Rodney’s Rant (which we did last week). https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/16526/
  7. The Montgomeries’ Rant (R) – An oldie but a goodie. A really old oldie, dating back to 1749, and very popular ever since. https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/4641/ There is a lot of ‘extra info’ in the link about who the Montgomeries were.

Right Shoulder/Left Shoulder Reels 
The walking dance, "New Year Jig", started with Rsh Figs of 8 and RSh reels. The details of the reels are in last week’s newsletter. 

Reels across the set
As well as along the sides, reels can also be danced across the set. In “Try as we Might”, the dancing couple cast into 2nd place, where they turn RH (right hand), so that they end up with the lady facing towards the 2nd lady (in 1st place), and the man facing towards 3rd man. They start Rsh reels from the middle position in the reel. In this video shows Rsh reels across the set at 1.03 .
https://youtu.be/1LanKzTOCLg

Pas de Basque 
This is the fifth and last step in Scottish country dancing. The others are: Slip step, skip change of step, strathspey travelling step, and strathspey setting step. 
We practised Pas de Basque in Reel tempo, 4/4, i.e. four even beats.  
  • Catherine demonstrated the step, which has three beats and a pause on the fourth beat. 
  • Then we tried the step, starting on the right foot:  R, L, R, pause; L, R, L, pause. 
  • Place R foot down; place L foot in front of the R foot (in 3rd position, T shape) and raise the R foot off the floor; shift your weight back onto the R foot again, and raise the L foot (pause); repeat, starting with the L foot. 
  • We started stepping it out slowly, and increasing in tempo, and finally doing it to the music.  
  • I think it helps if you don’t put your full weight on the foot that goes in front. Just try it as if you had a stone in your shoe, and you want to lift your foot off quickly. 
  • In the video you’ll see that the dancers kick the foot forward on the last beat (this is called the “jeté”), but for now it is enough to just pause on that beat, until you’ve got the hang of the rhythm. https://youtu.be/H0_lkdhMikI 
Pas de Basque, also called ‘setting’, is used as a ‘greeting’, so usually when you set to someone, the other person will set back to you. “Setting” once, consists of two bars - once starting on the R foot and once on the L foot. 

We danced “Reel Easy, which started with 1s and 2s linking hands and “setting" twice (= 4 bars) to their partners, then turning sideways, 1s facing 2s, and “set" twice again (4 bars). From there, they flow into a Rsh reel on the sides - 1s going down, 2s going up (2s were already facing up after the setting), 3s going up. 

The next dance, “First Love”, a strathspey (slow tempo) included a grand chain and a circle 6 hands round and back.

Grand chain
Alternating right hand and left hand, as in “Rights and Lefts”, but with six people. Remember the sequence of fitting 6 hand changes into 8 bars of music: Right, left, Ri-i-ight, Left, Right, Le-e-eft. Details in last week’s email.

Circle in Strathspey 
Remember the we usually circle towards the left first. Strathspey travelling step starts with the right foot. So to start the circle, the right foot has to cross the left to begin. Details in last week’s email.

Devisers’ Afternoon
The Capital City Club held a Devisers’ afternoon last Sunday, which was part of their 50th anniversary celebrations. Three sets were put through their paces with some quite challenging dances, and a couple of easier ones. Just three of us from Tawa attended (Maureen S, Helen M, and Désirée). Jeanette Watson was the MC for the afternoon: she taught Peter Beaumont’s “Fugue 32” and Xiaowen Yu’s “Ōtepoti Octagon" - both quite challenging. Iain Boyd, Rod Downey, and Romaine Butterfield each taught one of their recently devised dances ("Uyea Sound", "Link Road" and "Father Williams' Reel” respectively) and we completed the afternoon with an easier dance by Rod, “The Bunting Bee”. Oh, and there was an impressive afternoon tea as well.
