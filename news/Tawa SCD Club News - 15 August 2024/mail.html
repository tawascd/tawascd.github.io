What we did in the beginners‚Äô session:
We started with the same walking warm-up dance as last week, called ‚ÄúAll Right‚Äù, because all the formations use the Right hand. 
1-8 1s cross RH, cast down, 2s step up;  1s cross RH, cast up, 2s step down 
9-16  2s cross RH, cast up, 1s step down; 2s cross RH, cast down,1s step up
17-24 1s lead down for 4 bars, back up for 4 bars

Catherine reminded people that when crossing, they must not let their hands droop! Keep the hands at above waist level. 

After the stretches and foot and ankle exercises, we danced ‚ÄúLady Catherine Bruce‚Äôs Reel‚Äù. https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/3594/ We danced this last week also. 

Step practise
We reviewed the Strathspey travelling step: ‚ÄúStep, close, step, hop". Remember to slide the non-weight-bearing foot past the other, while keeping the foot close to the floor, with toes pointing down. Do not jerk the knee up. https://youtu.be/ykCf1wmYETk
Strathspey travelling step, backwards. Usually done with only two steps back. We practised dancing forward for two steps, then back for two steps. In the transition, ‚Äútidy away‚Äù the non-weight-bearing foot behind the calf of the other leg (as in the setting step). This step is done in the least two bars of an allemande.
Dancing strathspey in a circle: The steps are travelling steps, which usually move you forward. In a circle, however, you have to move sideways. In a circle, one usually circles to the left first, but one always starts with the right foot. So the right foot has to cross to the left to begin the circle. The trick here is that though your feet are going sideways, your body should still be facing into the circle. https://youtu.be/L_3BgigdQO4 Note in the video: the non-weight-bearing foot is ‚Äútidied away‚Äù behind the calf of the other leg.

New formation: ‚ÄúRights and Lefts"
This formation is done with two couples, and it works like a square.
With your Right hand, change places across the set with your partner, change Left hands with the other person along the side, then cross Right hands again with your partner, and change places Left hand along the side with the other person. 
  • You are going around in a square, you do not change direction (so no loops or twiddles in the corners). Catherine said ‚ÄúImagine you are walking around a square table" ‚Äì You wouldn‚Äôt twirl around each time you get to a corner. 
  • In a couple, one of you will be going clockwise, the other anti-clockwise. 
  • Across is always right hand;  along the side is always left hand
  • The hands should stay at above waist level - do not let them droop! 
  • When you have done the whole formation, you are back in the same position from where you started. 
  • In the video you will see that two of the people in each square finish with what is called a "courtesy turn‚Äù. Don‚Äôt worry about that just yet. Catherine will cover that in due course.

We danced "Rakes of Glasgow", a Strathspey, https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/5462/
1-8 1s & 2s dance Rights and Lefts
9-16 1s & 2s dance an allemande
17-24 1s dance down the middle and back up
25-32 6 hands round and back (i.e. 1s, 2, and 3s circle). 

This is a 3-couple dance, in a 4-couple set. Each couple dances the sequence twice - the first time starting from 1s place, the second time starting from 2nd place (as the allemande is a ‚Äúprogression‚Äù). When they have danced it twice, they finish in 3rd position, and they step down to 4th position, as the next couple starts from the top.  

We danced "Crabbit Shona‚Äù, a Jig(I, https://my.strathspey.org/dd/dance/13259/
This is a 2 -couple dance, which we danced in 3-couple sets. Each couple dances the sequence twice. The dance finishes with "Right and Lefts".

Ten Tawa members attended this dance last Saturday, plus on stage, Hilary in the band, and Joy as one of the three MCs. Robert wrote a report of the event for the Johnsonville Club newsletter (he is their secretary), and I have his permission to copy it here ‚Äì Thanks Robert.

Waikanae‚Äôs Annual Dance 2024 ‚Äì Best Hall in the Set?
Most of the set of local halls we use for our dances have their charms, but warmth is not one of them. It‚Äôs ok once the dancing starts but halls can be quite chilly at the start.
Waikanae‚Äôs Annual Dance on the night of Saturday 10 August was held in the stylish Paraparaumu Memorial Hall, designed in 1955 by Charles James Fearnley, an architect who adapted the ideas of European Modernism to domestic New Zealand design.
There was no chilly start this time, recent refurbishment of the hall by the local council included insulation, double glazing and a proper heating system and it was warm as toast right from the start. Dancers were removing coats and scarves and cardigans even before we hit the floor for the first dance of the night, Dame Margaret‚Äôs Jig, devised by Romaine Butterfield who was one of the dancers.
The hall‚Äôs wide span portal frame construction allowed three sets comfortably across the width - no collisions with the set next door when casting. There were seven sets at the start with a dozen Johnsonville dancers on the floor.
We had been promised live music by Hilary Ferral on fiddle and Jason Morris on accordion, and they were joined by Johnny Bourne on double bass, a great accompaniment to the night‚Äôs dancing. Two tiers of tartan, greenery and two large saltires set off the band on the spacious stage.
The first part of the programme ended with The Luckenbooth Brooch and then we all went off to a good-sized adjoining room to refuel with the generous supper that had been laid on by the Waikanae Club. The fruit salad, jelly and ice cream were very popular. After supper there were raffles and then we were back to dancing.
It was a good programme and so popular had been the dance that there were still six sets on the floor for the final dance of the night, The Reel of the Royal Scots. The 7:00pm start meant that the event was over by 10 o‚Äôclock, making less of a late night for the many who had travelled from Wellington to attend what had been a very enjoyable night of dancing.
